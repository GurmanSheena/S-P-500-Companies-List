    The Standard & Poor's 500 or in short S&P 500 is a stock popular market index that includes 500 of the largest publicly traded companies in the United States on the basics of market capitalization, liquidity, and sector representation. The index is considered a key indicator of the overall performance of the U.S stock market and the broader economy and is tracked by people in the USA and across the globe.

    Key Takeaways about S&P 500 Companies

    1. Diverse Sectors: The S&P 500 is designed to cover a wide range of industries, including technology, healthcare, financial services, consumer goods, energy, and others. The companies in the index are meant to represent the diverse U.S. economy.
    2. Market Capitalization: To be eligible for inclusion, a company typically needs a market capitalization of at least $8.2 billion. Larger companies, like Apple or Microsoft, have much higher market caps.
    3. Regular Adjustments: The composition of the S&P 500 is not fixed. Companies are added or removed based on their performance, size, and industry relevance. For example, if a company no longer meets the criteria or has a major decline in its market value, it can be replaced.

    Well-Known Companies: Some of the most recognizable and influential companies in the world are part of the S&P 500, such as: McDonald's, Johnson & Johnson, Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Tesla, Alphabet (Google), Berkshire Hathaway and others
